Planning workplace communication

Communication with supervisors, co-workers, contractors, clients and suppliers is an integral part of everyday work. It includes face-to-face interaction, radio or phone conversations as well as recorded instruction, procedures and protocols. 

By understanding where communication fits into your workplace and the implications of poor communication you can better ensure jobs go to plan.

Important aspects you should know and understand about the basics of communication in the workplace include:

  • Identifying instructions for communication

  • Personnel communication

  • Choosing suitable forms of communication

  • Identifying which communication methods suit your workplace

In order to understand how to effectively communicate, you must first identify what information needs to be communicated in the workplace.

Common information communicated on a construction worksite include:

  • Plans and specifications

  • Work instructions and requirements

  • Site meetings

  • Understanding safety signs

  • Safety requirements

  • Drawings or sketches

  • Policies and procedures

  • Personnel availability

Messages must be delivered clearly and appropriately to ensure the meaning and content is understood as intended. This means communication must be:

  • Clear

  • Relevant

  • Industry specific 

  • Delivered using the appropriate format.

When communication is not clear, misunderstandings can occur and errors can be made in a job.

Personnel communication

Communication is about presenting information to others in a way they will understand it. Who you are communicating with will determine the content and context of the conversation.

For example:

  • A casual discussion between co-workers will be different to receiving instruction from your supervisor.  

  • A verbal report you give to a customer about a completed job will be presented differently to the report you give to your supervisor about the same job. 

To ensure an effective communication process you as the sender must consider:

  • The message you want to send

  • What the best medium is to deliver your message 

  • Would voice, audio, writing a memo, sending an email or text message or a display of body language be a more suitable medium for the receiver of the message? 

  • What feedback will you seek to ensure the receiver understood your message?

The process is a cycle and you may need to adjust the message and/or the medium depending on the feedback you receive. 


Communication In The Industry

Communication in the plumbing services sector can take many forms:

  • Oral communication: Face to face discussions, mobile telephone conversations, loudspeaker. 

  • Written communication: Hydraulic plans, specifications, safety signage, incident reports, bulletin boards, text messages.

  • Visual communication: Flashing lights, barricade tapes, safety signage.




Choosing suitable communication

You must weigh up the most appropriate form of communication for the workplace context.

The pros and cons of the various forms of communication can be seen below:


Phone call (verbal)

Advantages:

  • Quick.

  • Instant response.

  • Receiver can be in a different location offsite.

  • Ability to share instant photos and discuss as required.

  • Can track and share exact location.

Disadvantages:

  • Unable to see and gauge the person’s understanding with non-verbal cues.

  • Conversation can go off track.

  • Possible security breaches.

  • Dedicated time to speak not required which may result in miscommunication.

Text message (non-verbal)

Advantages:

  • Quick.

  • Provides a record of the communication.

  • Can share photos instantly and discuss as required.

Disadvantages:

  • Can be misinterpreted.

  • Unable to see and gauge the person’s understanding with non-verbal cues.

Two-way radio (verbal)

Advantages:

  • Quick.

  • Instant response.

  • Receiver can be in a different location on the same site so face to face is not required.

Disadvantages:

  • May be difficult in a noisy environment.

  • Limits on who can be contacted.

  • Unable to see and gauge the person’s understanding with non-verbal cues.

E-mail (non-verbal)

Advantages:

  • Provides a record of the communication.

  • More formal/professional.

  • Can plan the communication to use the right words and structure to convey the message.

  • Can send the same message to multiple people/parties at the same time.

Disadvantages:

  • Takes longer to complete. 

  • Response may not be instantaneous.

  • Unable to see and gauge the person’s understanding with non-verbal cues.

Face-to-face Communication (verbal)

Advantages:

  • Able to ensure you have the persons attention.

  • Able to see the person’s body language to gauge their understanding.

  • Opportunity to paraphrase to ensure understanding.

  • Both parties can ask questions during the conversation rather than waiting till the end.

Disadvantages:

  • Difficult to hide or disguise your feelings about the response.

  • May appear confrontational.

  • Can be time consuming.

Oral communication

In this section you will learn the important aspects you should know and understand in order to use oral communication most effectively, including:

  • Clear oral communication

  • Adjusting communication to suit the situation

  • Confirming communication

  • Communicating with others orally and non-verbally in a clear and accurate manner


While most oral communication occurs almost without thinking, effective oral communication requires a more thoughtful approach. There are different forms of oral communication used in the workplace and each must be applied correctly, in the right context and at the right time to be effective.

Communication is a two-way street where you are responsible for your end of the process. Failure to provide clear oral communication may lead to miscommunication. 

As we saw in section 1.2, the communication process is one that that starts and ends with you. You are responsible for clearly speaking the information/message and you are responsible for ensuring it is understood by the other party.

To ensure verbal communication is effective you must:

Look

Make sure the person is looking at you.

  • By maintaining eye contact you can see if the person is engaged in what you are saying. 

  • You will ensure you have their attention.

  • You can see if they are understanding your message by their facial expressions and body language.

Think 

Choose words appropriate to the person and situation.

  • Use language that suits the circumstance, even when discussing the same subject with different people and/or in different situations

  • For example, how you speak to your supervisor about the job will be different to how you would explain things to the customer.

Consider how the words are spoken (tone, pace).

  • How you convey a message is as important as the words used. 

  • You are responsible for how the message is delivered. 

Reinforce words with other non-verbal communication.

  • Use gestures such as head nods, warm facial expressions and maintaining eye contact to encourage engagement from the other person.

Listen 

Listen actively. 

  • Avoid distractions and listen to the responses of the other person.

  • Try to concentrate on them and their message and not on how you are going to reply. It’s harder than you think.

Understand

Clarify understanding.

  • Paraphrase the message in your own words. This shows you are considering the other person’s viewpoint.

  • Ask questions to check you have understood the message. Open questions can be used to gather further information and closed questions to confirm the meaning. 

Reflect on the response.

  • Consider the message as a whole.

  • Only then should you consider an appropriate response.


Adjust communication to suit the situation

It is important to recognise when your message isn’t being understood so you can adjust your communication style to suit. Saying the same thing over and over in the same way and expecting someone to understand what you are saying is a waste of time. 

This may also require you to repeat (or paraphrase) what was said but in a different way.

Your direct communication style may be taken as offensive or misunderstood by others. 

Be aware of the part your non-verbal communication is playing. 

  • Does your body language, posture and facial expressions match your spoken word?

  • Non-verbal communication is vital to the overall message you are speaking. 

Be aware of using technical/industry specific jargon.

  • When speaking with customers jargon may be confusing and misguiding.

Ensure you are adjusting your wording, your tone and your gestures to avoid a communication breakdown.

Some other things to consider include:

  • Use verbal and non-verbal communication

  • Make allowances for those with hearing difficulties

  • Use industry-specific terminology only where appropriate

  • Speak clearly and slowly

  • Move to a quieter location

  • Simplify your language

By understanding that every situation and every person is unique you can learn to adjust your own communication style to ensure the correct message is delivered in the best way.

Toolbox Talks

To enable effective consultation and communication many building and construction sites use Toolbox Talks. 

A Toolbox Talk is an informal health and safety meeting that focuses on topics related to the workplace or job, such as hazards, safe work practices and how the organisation is minimising risks. 

Toolbox talks show the worker onsite that supervisors and managers are willing to have an open dialogue. Difficult-to-discuss project and safety issues are discussed and strategies to address the issues are outlined. 

WorkSafe offers a number of handbooks and resources to aid in communicating health and safety information in Toolbox Talks.

Confirm communication

The essence of communication involves two things and these may not always align.

If there was a miscommunication of any kind in what you have said then it is you who is responsible. 

It is important to confirm that your message was not only received but understood.

  • Just because someone listened to you does not mean they understood you. 

  • Just because someone says they understand you doesn’t mean they understood the meaning you intended.

  • Just because they say they have no questions doesn’t mean they don’t.

Look for the non-verbal cues. Do they look confused? Do they look like they have understood you? 

Ask follow-up questions to check for understanding using open and closed questions

Open questions require a detailed response to demonstrate understanding. 

Closed questions require only a yes or no response.

While non-verbal communication is used to enhance verbal communication, the nature of noisy construction sites means plumbers are often forced to communicate using only non-verbal communication.

Types of non-verbal communication

Non-verbal communication or body language is the unspoken part of communication. Being aware of non-verbal communication you are displaying is important to ensure your message is not misunderstood.

Its use must be considered as much as the actual words used.

Non-verbal communication includes:

  • Personal space

  • Positioning of hands and body

  • Posture

  • Attention on the other person

  • Gestures

  • Facial expressions

Non-verbal communication may be used on its own to convey a message or in conjunction with verbal communication to add strength to your spoken message. 

It can:

  • Enhance the spoken word if used correctly

For example, telling your co-worker they’ve done a good job while smiling at them can increase the impact of your message.

  • Contradict the spoken message

For example, quoting a customer and telling them you’ll look after them while you are smirking may indicate you may not be telling the truth.

  • Be a substitute for a verbal message

For example, making eye contact and gesturing for someone to come over to you can be used when you are working on a noisy building site.

  • Accent or underline a verbal message 

For example, banging the phone on the table when discussing the increased incidents occurring due to the distraction of mobile phones being used on site can underline the importance of your message.

Adjust non-verbal communication

To avoid misunderstandings and communicate effectively, it is important to understand how to use and interpret non-verbal communication (or body language).

By knowing how important non-verbal communication is in the way you deliver your message, you can then consider how to adjust your communication to project and convey the correct message.

Non-verbal communication requires your full focus on the moment-to-moment experience. You must give your full attention to the communication so you can pick up on the non-verbal cues, the subtleties and expression of non-verbal communication returned to you.

It is up to you to read the situation and adjust your response to engage the other person and ensure they understand your message as intended.

Some examples include:

  • If someone looks confused then you have the opportunity to reword or rephrase things, or ask them about their understanding.

  • If someone looks disinterested try adjusting your tone or actively involving them in the conversation.

  • When someone is trying to distance themselves from you be mindful you may be entering their personal space, so take a step back before continuing.

Visual communication and signage

The construction industry, like many others, relies on visual communication and signage to reinforce safety requirements. Highly visible safety signs have become a mandatory requirement as they help prevent injuries and ensure staff and visitors are aware of any dangerous hazards. Each sign delivers a clear meaning with simple graphics and wording.

Below are examples of some safety signs that comply with Australian occupational health and safety standards.

Mandatory Signs

Depicts a specific action that must take place on site.

Consists of:

  • White background

  • White text/image

  • Blue circle that depicts the mandatory action


Prohibition signs

Depicts a specific action not permitted on site.

Consists of:

  • White background

  • Black text/image

  • Red circle with a line through an image displaying a particular action


Warning Signs

Depicts and warns of a hazard or potential hazard. Designed to improve safety. 

Consists of:

  • Black text/image

  • Yellow background with a triangle addressing the danger

Fire safety signs

Depicts the location of fire extinguishers and other safety equipment in the event of a fire. 

Consists of:

  • White text/image

  • Red background


Emergency Information Signs

Depicts the location of emergency facilities and equipment. 

Consists of:

  • White text/image

  • Green background


Danger Signs

Depicts and warns of life-threatening hazards, potential hazards or dangerous goods.

Consists of:

  • Black text 

  • Red oval on the top with the word DANGER written in white

Reading and interpreting technical information

Before starting any works, it is important for plumbers to be aware of the relevant Australian Standards, codes of practise and any jurisdictional requirements that may apply.

These requirements regulate the way in which works are carried out.

An example of how the Australian standards are used is:

You have been asked by your supervisor to go to a construction site and work with the excavator driver to excavate a trench from a new dwelling to the sewer connection point. 

To ensure the trench is completed correctly, (allowing for bedding materials) you will need to access AS/NZ 3500.2: Plumbing and Drainage Standard to determine the minimum bedding requirements for the drain. Then you will need to make the excavation large enough to allow for the required bedding material to ensure the installation is compliant.

National Construction Code (NCC) 

The NCC is a uniform set of technical provisions for plumbing, drainage and building works in Australia. It provides the minimum necessary requirements for:

  • safety and health

  • amenity and accessibility

  • building works in existing buildings

  • sustainability

  • liveability of new buildings

  • construction.

An example is The Plumbing Code of Australia (PCA). This is volume 3 of the NCC and applies to plumbing and drainage for all classes of buildings. 

A plumber will satisfy the NCC by ensuring installations comply with the various Australian Standards that exist. 

Note: 

There may be occasions when it is simply not possible to comply with the Australian Standards. This is when the NCC can be used to find alternate paths to compliance. This is not a common practise, and in most instances the Australian Standards are considered the go to documentation for plumbers in the industry.

Completing Regulators' Documentation

The Victorian Building Authority (VBA) regulates Victoria's plumbing practitioners and plumbing work. This includes the requirement to be registered or licenced as well as the completion of documentation.

While most regulatory documentation contains simple instructions for completion, the documents must be completed correctly and fully for safety, compliance and quality assurance purposes.

As a written form of communication, regulatory documentation forms part of the evidence required for completion of works and the formal notification of events.

Safety documentation

Before any works are completed in any workplace a risk assessment should be conducted to assess the risks that may be present and to eliminate or reduce those risks as far as reasonably practical. This is crucial in the construction industry. 

A job safety analysis (JSA) and a safe work method statement (SWMS) are used to carry out a risk assessment in the construction industry. Both documents provide a process to assess jobs step by step to either eliminate or reduce the risks. 

The difference between the two documents is a SWMS is a legislated document and must be completed whenever one (1) or more of 19 specific construction tasks – defined as high risk construction work (HRCW) – are performed. These tasks are listed below and can also be sourced directly from WorkSafe Victoria’s website. 

For all other situations a JSA will generally be used to assess the risk of a job.

To complete either the JSA or SWMS you must ensure each step of the task or job is well documented and the potential hazards for each step identified. 

Then, controls can be listed and put in place to ensure the hazards are either eliminated (the ideal outcome) or reduced to a level that is considered acceptable.

Compliance certificates

Certain types of plumbing work require a compliance certificate on completion. Only a licenced plumber may issue a compliance certificate and it certifies that work complies with the prescribed plumbing standards. 

A registered plumber must not complete any work requiring a compliance certificate unless they are working under the supervision of a licensed plumber.

Record and report on work activities

As well as regulatory documentation, it is important to understand how to record and report on events/incidents that occur in the workplace. Most companies will have their own forms to be completed for different aspects of each job and when a more formal approach to reporting is required.

On the job, many plumbers make calculations and record information on notepaper or in a notebook for personal use and to refer to later. It is important to make sure you can read your own notes and they contain enough detail for you to complete the company forms later. 

The use of pre-printed forms ensures the necessary information is recorded and a register kept, complying with legal requirements. 

Plumbers will typically allow time after a job is completed to record information about the job. This includes certificates of compliance, incident reports and invoices.

Records must also be kept in relation to safety concerns and incidents on a work site. Serious incidents however must be immediately reported to the site supervisor and the relevant authorities and or regulators.
